The Courage to Say No, Without the Guilt

How I’ve learned to navigate sex on my own terms

Madhuri Sastry
Jul 3

But guilt kept me up for a long time, and eventually, I became angry with myself. It wasn’t the first time I had felt shitty about saying no to sex. Not for nothing: I have a strong mother, one who would be alarmed that I was even validating the idea of consent by guilt. I live in New York, and am not as submerged in toxic desi culture as I used to be. (The toxicity is different here. I learned the phrase “If you don’t put out, someone else will ¯\_(ツ)_/¯” on one of the Housewives shows.) And yet, the guilt was so consuming and resilient. For months after, I thought about why saying no was hard for me, especially when it came to sex.

Saying no to sex — or to babies, or even simply to plans — is subversive. It goes against everything we have been taught. It’s an exercise of autonomy and self-preservation. A radical act of self-care that acknowledges that we do, in fact, have a self to care for.

Growing up, taboo, stigma, and shame around our bodies and sex was just a way of life. In an all-girls school, we were ashamed of our period: that-what-must-not-be named. Even a sanitary pad was not a “sanitary pad.” Like once, a girl conspiratorially whispered to me, eyes narrowed, “Do you have an uncle on you?” (Don’t ask me the etymology.) In some places, Indian women are banished to huts when they have their period — they’re considered “impure” during this time. (Are dudes banished when they have hemorrhoids? I think not. Every man with hemorrhoids is lying on his bed, with a wife soaking his asshole in warm water and witch hazel or whatever out of a sense of duty.)

Saying no to sex is subversive. It goes against everything we have been taught.

No one told us that our needs mattered. So, we didn’t learn about consent, female pleasure, masturbation — or anything other than penile-vaginal intercourse for the purposes of reproduction (i.e., a basic biology class). “I didn’t masturbate till I was 20,” my Brown friend confided over a glass of rosé. “I felt suuuper guilty, and ashamed,” she shivered a little and — understandably — drained her wine in one sip. “Even now, the idea of taking pleasure into my own hands, is so… weird.”

“Meanwhile I see at least one dude masturbating in public on a daily basis,” I shrug.

Indian women are still considered “chattel.” Let’s be honest: Wedding ceremonies are just glorified property-transfer parties (her ownership from father to husband). Some laws and court decisions have come around, and say maybe they’re not? But many others leave the notion of women’s autonomy open to debate: marital rape, for example, is not a crime. And as exhibit A that India’s laws are by and for men: The general view is that denying sex in a marriage is “cruelty” and can be grounds for divorce. It should go without saying, but… women also want to have sex, of course, and can use the law to divorce their husbands, but conceptualizing sex as something that is “owed” is just wrong.

And yet, it is accepted. We owe our bodies to men.

One of the most insidious effects of toxic desi culture is how it makes women feel guilty and ashamed for saying no. We have never been taught to lay boundaries. If we could’ve said no, we’d be proving that we are actual human beings with all kinds of feelings, and a sense of self-determination. How, then, could we be controlled, exploited? So, we are taught to say yessir, haanjisir, or else we will be browbeaten into it. After all, no is a yes not yet forced out.

Sexually, women are “conquests.” I grew up on Bollywood films that normalized and romanticized stalking. There’s a line in a popular Bollywood song in the film Josh starring former Miss World Aishwarya Rai and Shah Rukh Khan. He’s trying to wear his love interest down, and is whining to his sister about its futility. She consoles him: yeh uska style hoinga, hoton pe na dil mein haan hoinga. “This must be her style; there’s a no on her lips but there must be a yes in her heart.”

It makes me think of being stalked myself, the existential fear of being in a wide-open space. Of conversations with women trading “what I did when I thought my body was in danger,” stories (“I lied and said I had pepper spray!”) Of teenage girls who were never taught about consent, and are never given an opportunity to learn it as women. In the critically acclaimed film Lipstick Under My Burkha, Konkona Sen Sharma’s character, Shirin, is frighteningly real. Shirin complains that “it burns” when she has sex with her husband. He obviously doesn’t care, and so she suffers through the dutiful sex. He also doesn’t use a condom, forcing her to get multiple abortions. Shirin’s gynecologist shames her for this. Shirin is too ashamed and proud to be vulnerable. So her friends think that surely, she — a successful salesperson — actually had reproductive rights. Little do they know that when she’s offered a career opportunity, her husband rapes her, so she doesn’t lose sight of her “place.”

Patriarchal guilt and shame — especially around women’s bodies — is indiscriminate. How many strong women have been assaulted, how many educated women raped? How many who “should have known better” cover up burns and bruises their partners gave them before they go to parent-teacher meetings?

How many said no a thousand times, and weren’t heard?

I think of the women who have suffered acid attacks, burned for saying no to men. Those pioneers of autonomy, pavers of the way. I remember my stomach tightening into knots upon seeing pictures of their charred faces in newspapers. It frightened me terribly, the hatred, the vindictiveness.

What if it happened to me? Any one of the very real, violent consequences of saying no?

There’s drinking the patriarchy Kool-Aid, and there’s being submerged in it for so long your skin absorbs it and turns it into blood. A switch flipped after I got married. All the conventions and contradictions I internalized, all the expectations of me as a woman and my performance of this “wife” role, and all my insecurities are heightened in the context of my marriage. It’s what makes me feel guilty when I refuse sex with my partner or for wanting a career more than children.

I’m immensely privileged to be able to use the word no. I’m in a safe, loving relationship where I initiate, enjoy, and refuse sex. I can practice agency, autonomy, and setting boundaries until one day, maybe the guilt won’t feel visceral.
